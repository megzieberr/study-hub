✍️ How to Write Each Type of Answer

💡 Mark allocation = amount of detail needed

  • (1 mark) = one precise scientific word or single fact
  • (2 marks) = full definition OR two clearly linked points
  • (3–4 marks) = "explain" or "describe" = that many distinct, accurate points
  • (5–8 marks) = diagram (heading + correct structure + all labels + functions) OR extended flow diagram with arrows
  • (10 marks) = Q1 column match = 10 correct matches, 1 mark each

✍️ Answer-writing rules that gain marks

  • Use scientific terms always — "oestrogen" not "the female hormone"
  • For "explain" = state the cause → mechanism → result
  • For "describe" = say what you see/what happens, step by step
  • For "define" = give the complete textbook definition (cause + effect)
  • Diagrams: heading is ALWAYS required for marks, neat leader lines, correct labels
  • Flow diagrams: arrows must show direction; all sequential steps must be in order
  • Never leave a blank — partial credit is possible for partial answers

Reproduction in Flowering Plants

Structure · Pollination · Fertilisation · Seed & Fruit Formation · Sexual vs Asexual · Germination · Auxins · Seed Banks

🌍 Why this matters — putting the theory in context

Flowering plants (angiosperms) are the most successful group of plants on Earth — over 300 000 species. Their success comes from an evolved reproductive system combining sexual reproduction (producing variation through seeds) and asexual reproduction (producing clones quickly). Farmers, geneticists, and food security experts all depend on understanding these mechanisms. South Africa's seed bank (National Plant Genetic Resources Centre) collects wild plant seeds to protect against future crop failure. The IEB tests whether you understand the WHY behind these structures and processes — not just the names.

🐝 2. Pollination — Types, Mechanisms, Advantages and Disadvantages

Definition and Context

Pollination = the transfer of pollen grains from the anther (of one flower) to the stigma (of the same or a different flower of the same species).

Pollination is NOT fertilisation — it is only the delivery of pollen. After landing on the stigma, pollen germinates and grows a pollen tube down the style before fertilisation can occur. Think of it like the pollen being a "delivery package" — it still has to be unpacked and the sperm nucleus has to travel to the egg.

⚠️
Classic trap (IEB True/False): "Fertilisation occurs at the stigma" → FALSE. Pollination occurs at the stigma. Fertilisation occurs inside the ovule when the sperm nucleus fuses with the egg cell.

🌱 3. Fertilisation in Flowering Plants — Step by Step

The full journey from pollen landing to zygote forming

Once pollen lands on the stigma, the process of getting the male gamete to the egg inside the ovule takes hours to days. This is a multi-step journey through the style:

1. Pollen grain lands on sticky stigma surface (= POLLINATION)
↓
2. Pollen grain absorbs water from the stigma fluid and GERMINATES — pollen tube starts to grow outward
↓
3. Pollen tube grows DOWN through the style (guided by chemical attractants toward the ovule)
↓
4. Pollen tube enters the ovule through a small pore called the MICROPYLE
↓
5. The pollen tube releases the SPERM NUCLEUS (male gamete) into the ovule
↓
6. SPERM NUCLEUS fuses with EGG CELL NUCLEUS → ZYGOTE (2n diploid) forms — this is FERTILISATION
↓
7. Zygote divides by mitosis and differentiates → develops into the EMBRYO (the future plant)
↓
8. OVULE → SEED (embryo + food store + testa). OVARY WALL (pericarp) → FRUIT
📌
IEB 2020 memo — how fertilisation occurs in flowering plants (2 marks): "Sperm (from pollen tube) fuses with ovum/egg cell — must have the concept of FUSION for the zygote mark."

What is a FRUIT? (IEB 2020 — asked repeatedly)

A fruit is a developed/ripened ovary wall (pericarp) that surrounds one or more seeds. Evidence that something is a fruit:

  • It contains seeds
  • It grew from an ovary
  • Seeds developed from ovules
  • It is a product of sexual reproduction
⚠️
A tomato = fruit (contains seeds, grew from ovary). A potato = NOT a fruit (it is a modified stem/tuber — asexual reproduction). A pea pod = fruit (pod is the ovary wall; peas inside are the seeds).
🌱 5. Germination

Definition and conditions required

Germination = the resumption of growth of the embryo inside a dormant seed when favourable conditions occur.

Three essential conditions (tested in MCQ and True/False):

ConditionWhy it is neededWhat happens without it
WaterActivates enzymes; converts stored insoluble food into soluble form for transport; softens testa; enters through micropyle; provides medium for biochemical reactions; allows turgor for cell expansionSeed remains dormant; metabolic processes cannot begin
OxygenNeeded for aerobic respiration to produce ATP energy for growth; the embryo has no photosynthesis yetNo energy for cell division or growth; seed dies or remains dormant
Correct temperatureEnzymes work optimally within a narrow temperature range (usually 15–30°C for most crops); metabolic reactions have minimum activation energy requirementsToo cold: enzymes too slow; too hot: enzymes denatured; seed fails to germinate

Note: some seeds also require light, or a period of cold (vernalisation), or fire (some proteas), or passage through an animal's gut to break seed dormancy before they will germinate.

Sequence of germination:

Seed absorbs water → testa softens and splits → radicle (root) emerges first (anchors plant, absorbs water and minerals) → plumule (shoot) emerges and grows upward (phototropism) → first true leaves unfold → photosynthesis begins → seedling is now independent.

Terminology traps:
  • "Tissue culture produces many small plantlets" = plant cloning / asexual reproduction (IEB 2013 column match: tissue culture = plant cloning)

IEB 2014 — Pros and Cons debate on asexual reproduction in peaches (4 marks):

Pros: "Cost-effective and time-saving since grafted/cloned saplings grow faster than seedlings, reaching productivity earlier. Product (peach) quality is consistent for food markets."

Cons: "Artificial process involving cost. Reduction of genetic variation/fertility rates. Can result in huge crop loss from the same disease/pests (no variation to confer resistance)."

🧪 7. Auxins — Plant Hormones

What are auxins, what do they do, and how are they tested?

Auxins are plant hormones (chemical growth regulators). The IEB directly linked them to human hormones: "Name the hormone in humans that has a similar role to plant auxins" → Growth Hormone (GH/STH/somatotropin) (IEB 2020). Both promote growth/elongation of cells.

  • Auxins cause cells to elongate by loosening cell walls — more water enters → cells expand
  • Phototropism: auxins move to the shaded side of a stem → shaded cells elongate more → stem bends TOWARDS the light
  • Asexual reproduction via cuttings: auxins stimulate cells in the cutting to undergo mitosis, then differentiate into root, stem, and leaf cells → new plant grows. This is why rooting powder (containing auxin) is applied to stem cuttings.
  • IEB 2020 — role of auxins in asexual reproduction: "Asexual reproduction involves causing plant somatic cells to produce new plant organs / auxins cause cells to undergo mitosis / auxins cause differentiation to produce root, stem, and leaf cells / new plants."
  • Polyploid tomatoes: auxins can stimulate mitosis in somatic cells to produce plants with more chromosome sets → larger, more nutritious, often seedless fruits
🏦 8. Seed Banks and Genetic Diversity

Why are seed banks important? (IEB 2020 — 2 marks)

  • Store seeds to re-establish crop plants in case of natural disaster, war, drought, climate change, or total crop failure
  • Store seeds from wild plant relatives that have greater genetic diversity than commercial varieties
  • Re-introduce genetic variation (biodiversity) into cultivated plants that have become genetically uniform through inbreeding
  • Ensure long-term food security by preserving diverse crop varieties for adaptation to future environmental changes

Why heirloom varieties have LOWER genetic diversity than wild relatives (IEB 2020): "Heirloom varieties developed from small groups of plants breeding with one another → many characteristics lost / increase in homozygosity / inbreeding. Whereas wild plants can be pollinated from any individual in the area → random breeding → higher genetic diversity."

The Endocrine System

All glands · All hormones · Homeostasis · Negative feedback · Blood glucose · Thyroid · Sperm production

🌍 Context — why does the endocrine system exist?

Your body faces a constant challenge: the outside world changes (temperature, food availability, stress, injury) but your cells can only survive within a very narrow set of internal conditions. The endocrine system is the body's long-distance chemical communication network. Glands release hormones directly into the bloodstream — essentially sending chemical messages that travel to target organs anywhere in the body. Unlike the nervous system (fast, electrical, short-lived), hormones work slowly but sustain their effects over hours or days. Without the endocrine system: blood sugar would spike fatally after every meal; puberty wouldn't trigger; the body couldn't respond to stress; and the menstrual cycle wouldn't exist. Every single function on your demarcation depends on hormones.

🔄 3. Negative Feedback — The Universal Control Mechanism

How negative feedback works — general model

Negative feedback is the body's thermostat principle. It is called "negative" because the response opposes and reverses the original change. Every time a level deviates from its set point, sensors detect this and trigger a corrective response that brings it back. The response then "switches off" once the set point is restored.

NORMAL SET POINT (e.g. blood glucose ~4–8 mmol/L; thyroxin at normal level)
↓ Stimulus
Level deviates — rises ABOVE or falls BELOW the set point
↓ Detection
Receptor/sensor detects deviation (e.g. pancreatic β-cells detect high glucose; hypothalamus detects low thyroxin)
↓ Signal
Control centre (often hypothalamus/pituitary) releases a corrective hormone
↓ Response
Effector (target organ) responds to reverse the deviation
↓ Restoration
Level returns to set point → response is "switched off" → NEGATIVE FEEDBACK LOOP COMPLETE

🩸 4. Blood Glucose Regulation — In Full Detail

Insulin and glucagon — how they work together

Blood glucose must stay within approximately 4–8 mmol/L. Too high (hyperglycaemia) = diabetes complications — blood vessels and nerves are damaged. Too low (hypoglycaemia) = the brain is starved of fuel and you lose consciousness. The pancreas monitors blood glucose levels minute-by-minute and releases the correct hormone. This is one of the most precisely controlled systems in the body.

🔻 High blood glucose → INSULIN

  1. After eating: blood glucose rises above set point
  2. β-cells of Islets of Langerhans detect high glucose
  3. β-cells secrete INSULIN into bloodstream
  4. Insulin binds receptors on liver + muscle cells
  5. Cell membranes become more permeable to glucose → glucose enters cells
  6. Liver/muscle convert glucose → glycogen (GLYCOGENESIS — stored)
  7. Rate of cellular respiration increases → more glucose burned for energy
  8. Blood glucose drops back to normal → β-cells detect this → insulin secretion decreases
  9. Negative feedback complete

🔺 Low blood glucose → GLUCAGON

  1. During fasting or exercise: blood glucose drops below set point
  2. α-cells of Islets of Langerhans detect low glucose
  3. α-cells secrete GLUCAGON into bloodstream
  4. Glucagon binds receptors on liver cells
  5. Liver converts stored glycogen → glucose (GLYCOGENOLYSIS)
  6. Glucose released into bloodstream
  7. Blood glucose rises back to normal → α-cells detect this → glucagon secretion decreases
  8. Negative feedback complete

Role of adrenalin in blood glucose:

During stress/fright/intense exercise, the adrenal medulla releases adrenalin, which rapidly mobilises glucose from glycogen stores in the liver (same as glucagon but faster and through a different pathway). 🌍 Context

Human reproduction integrates everything: the endocrine system controls every step (hormones trigger puberty, drive the menstrual cycle, maintain pregnancy); meiosis produces the gametes; fertilisation restores the diploid number; and a nine-month developmental programme produces a new human being. ♂ 1. Male Reproductive System — All Structures

Every structure, its function, and what the IEB asks about it

🔵 Testes (singular: testis)
Produce sperm by spermatogenesis AND secrete testosterone. Located in the scrotum (outside body) to keep temperature 2–3°C below core body temperature — essential because spermatogenesis is impaired by normal body temperature (37°C). The two functions are in different cells: seminiferous tubules make sperm; Leydig (interstitial) cells make testosterone.
IEB: "D: produces sperm/male gametes/testosterone"
🔵 Seminiferous tubules
Highly coiled tubes packed inside the testes where spermatogenesis occurs. Lined by Sertoli cells (which nourish developing sperm) and spermatogonia (stem cells). Length increases dramatically at puberty under FSH stimulation (IEB 2019 graph question).
Function: "site of spermatogenesis / gamete production"
🔵 Epididymis
Tightly coiled tube on the posterior surface of each testis. Sperm travel here from seminiferous tubules and spend 10–14 days maturing — gaining the ability to swim (motility) and ability to fertilise an egg. Also stores sperm until ejaculation.
IEB 2014 label C: epididymis. "Maturation AND storage of sperm"
🔵 Vas deferens (sperm duct)
Muscular tube that carries mature sperm from the epididymis up and over the bladder to the urethra during ejaculation. CUT during vasectomy (sterilisation procedure). About 30 cm long.
IEB 2014 label A: vas deferens/sperm duct
🔵 Seminal vesicles
Two pouch-like glands at the back of the bladder that secrete a thick alkaline fluid rich in FRUCTOSE (energy source for sperm motility) and prostaglandins. Contribute ~60% of semen volume. The alkalinity neutralises the slight acidity of the vagina.
IEB 2014: "produces/secretes fluid to promote sperm movement / nutrition / correct pH"
🔵 Prostate gland
Walnut-sized gland at the BASE OF THE BLADDER, surrounding the upper urethra. Secretes an alkaline milky fluid that enhances sperm motility and helps neutralise vaginal acidity. IEB asks: "This structure at the junction of bladder and urethra secretes fluid contributing to semen" = prostate gland.
Answer = B (prostate gland) in 2013 MCQ
🔵 Cowper's gland (Bulbourethral)
Two pea-sized glands below the prostate. Secrete clear pre-ejaculatory fluid that lubricates the urethra and neutralises any remaining urine acidity before ejaculation. Can contain a small number of sperm — why withdrawal method is unreliable.
🔵 Urethra
Carries both urine and semen out of the body (NEVER simultaneously — a sphincter valve prevents mixing). The ejaculatory ducts (from vas deferens + seminal vesicles) join the urethra. During erection the urinary sphincter closes.
IEB 2014 label B: urethra
🔵 Penis
Organ of sexual intercourse and urination. Contains three cylinders of erectile tissue that fill with blood during sexual arousal (erection). During ejaculation, semen is forcibly expelled through the urethra. External genitals = penis + scrotum.
🔵 Scrotum
External skin pouch containing the testes. Holds testes outside the body cavity — maintains testicular temperature ~2–3°C BELOW core body temperature. In cold conditions: scrotal muscles contract (cremasteric reflex) pulling testes closer to body. In heat: muscles relax, testes descend away from body.

🔄 3. Spermatogenesis — Step-by-Step

Production of sperm — from stem cell to mature gamete

Spermatogenesis occurs continuously from puberty (~12 years) throughout a man's life (into his 70s–80s). The full process takes ~64–72 days. It is stimulated by FSH (from pituitary) and testosterone (from Leydig cells). Sertoli cells in the tubule walls nourish and protect developing sperm.

↓ Mitosis (stem cells always remain; this makes sperm production continuous)
Primary spermatocyte (2n — grows larger; DNA replicates)
↓ Meiosis I (reduction division — chromosome number halved)

↓ Meiosis II (equational division)

↓ Spermiogenesis (NOT a cell division — maturation/differentiation of spermatids)

📌
Key comparison: 1 primary spermatocyte → 4 equal, functional sperm cells. This is fundamentally different from oogenesis (1 primary oocyte → only 1 functional egg + 2–3 non-functional polar bodies).
♀ 4. Female Reproductive System — All Structures

Every structure, function, and exam connection

🔴 Ovaries
Produce egg cells (oogenesis) and secrete oestrogen (from Graafian follicle) and progesterone (from corpus luteum). Each ovary is about the size of an almond. Contains thousands of primary follicles at birth — these are the lifetime supply of potential eggs.
IEB: "organ containing Graafian follicle" = ovary
🔴 Graafian follicle
A large, fluid-filled sac in the ovary that develops from a primary follicle. Contains a maturing secondary oocyte surrounded by follicular cells. Secretes oestrogen. At ovulation (~day 14) the Graafian follicle RUPTURES and releases the egg. The rupture IS ovulation.
IEB: "Rupture of Graafian follicle" = OVULATION
🔴 Corpus luteum
Forms from the ruptured Graafian follicle AFTER ovulation (follicular cells reorganise into luteal cells). Secretes HIGH amounts of PROGESTERONE and some oestrogen. Maintains the endometrium. If no fertilisation → degenerates into corpus albicans → progesterone drops → menstruation. If fertilisation → HCG from embryo keeps corpus luteum alive → progesterone stays high → no menstruation = pregnancy.
IEB: "Progesterone remains high during pregnancy" = corpus luteum maintained by HCG
🔴 Oviduct (Fallopian tube)
Muscular tube ~10 cm long connecting each ovary to the uterus. Lined internally with cilia that sweep the egg towards the uterus. Peristaltic contractions help move the egg. The ampulla (widest part) is the most common site of fertilisation — sperm swim up to meet the egg here.
IEB: "Most common site of fertilisation" = oviduct (NOT uterus)
🔴 Uterus (womb)
Thick-walled, pear-shaped muscular organ. Site of embryo implantation. Houses and nourishes the developing foetus for 9 months. Labour contractions originate from its smooth muscle. Inner lining = endometrium (shed during menstruation; rebuilt each cycle).
IEB 2014: "Labour initiated by contraction of muscles in this organ" = uterus
🔴 Endometrium
The inner lining of the uterus. Thickens under oestrogen (days 6–13) then under progesterone (days 15–28) to prepare a rich, vascular bed for implantation. If no implantation → endometrium breaks down and is shed during menstruation (days 1–5). If fertilisation → embryo implants into this layer.
IEB: "Attachment of developing embryo into this lining" = implantation into endometrium
🔴 Cervix
The narrow lower neck/opening of the uterus projecting into the vagina. Produces mucus that changes through the cycle: thin and watery at ovulation (allows sperm in) → thick and hostile at other times (blocks sperm). The Mirena/progesterone injection also works by thickening cervical mucus. Dilates to 10 cm during childbirth.
🔴 Vagina
Muscular tube (~9 cm) from cervix to external opening. Receives the penis during intercourse (semen deposited here). Birth canal — baby exits through vagina. Slightly acidic pH (bacteria keep it healthy). Sperm are alkaline — semen's alkalinity helps neutralise vaginal acidity.
IEB 2013 MCQ: "vagina — fertilisation" = INCORRECT. Fertilisation is in oviduct.
🔴 Mammary glands
Milk-producing glands in the breasts. Stimulated to produce milk (LACTATION) by PROLACTIN from the anterior pituitary. Milk RELEASE triggered by OXYTOCIN (posterior pituitary) when the baby suckles. Also provides maternal antibodies to the baby (passive immunity).
IEB 2013: "gland that produces milk and supplies antibodies to baby" = mammary gland
🔄 5. Oogenesis — Step-by-Step

Production of egg cells — unique features you must know

Unlike spermatogenesis (continuous from puberty), oogenesis begins in the FEMALE FOETUS and is arrested at TWO points. All the primary oocytes a woman will ever have (~1–2 million) are present at birth. By puberty this drops to ~400 000. Only about 400 eggs are ever ovulated in a lifetime.

Oogonium (2n) — multiplies by mitosis in foetal ovary before birth (only)
↓ Begins meiosis I — then ARRESTED at Prophase I (before birth)
Primary oocyte (2n) — present at birth in primary follicles; can remain dormant for up to 50 years
↓ ONE per cycle completes meiosis I at puberty (stimulated by FSH)
Secondary oocyte (n) + 1st polar body (n, non-functional, discarded)
↓ ARRESTED at Metaphase II — released at OVULATION (day ~14)
Secondary oocyte released from ovary at ovulation → travels down oviduct
↓ Meiosis II completed ONLY if fertilisation occurs
Mature ovum (n) + 2nd polar body (non-functional) + polar bodies from 1st polar body
↓ Result

⚠️
Why do polar bodies form? Oogenesis uses UNEQUAL cell division — one daughter cell gets almost ALL the cytoplasm (food stores, organelles) → becomes the egg. The other gets almost none → becomes the polar body. This ensures the egg has maximum resources for early embryo development. This is the opposite of spermatogenesis which uses EQUAL cell division (all 4 spermatids are the same size).

🤱 6. Fertilisation, Implantation, and Pregnancy

Timeline from fertilisation to birth

Fertilisation
Day 14 (at ovulation)
Sperm reaches the secondary oocyte in the OVIDUCT. The acrosome releases enzymes digesting the zona pellucida. ONE sperm enters → zona pellucida hardens (blocks polyspermy). Sperm nucleus fuses with egg nucleus → ZYGOTE (2n). Meiosis II of secondary oocyte is completed here.
Cleavage
Days 14–21
Zygote undergoes rapid mitosis without growing (cleavage): 2-cell → 4-cell → 8-cell → morula (solid ball of cells) → blastocyst (hollow ball with inner cell mass). Travels down oviduct to uterus (cilia + peristalsis).
Implantation
Day ~21 (7 days post-fertilisation)
Blastocyst attaches to and sinks into the endometrium of the uterus. Outer trophoblast cells develop chorionic villi which burrow into the endometrium. Chorionic villi immediately begin producing HCG (human chorionic gonadotropin) — the hormone detected by home pregnancy tests. IEB 2014: "Attachment of developing embryo sinking into endometrium" = IMPLANTATION.
Placenta formation
Weeks 4–12
Chorionic villi interdigitate with maternal blood supply → placenta forms. Functions: (1) exchange of O₂, glucose, amino acids, vitamins, antibodies from mother → foetus; (2) CO₂ and waste products from foetus → mother; (3) barrier against most bacteria and some drugs (not all!); (4) HCG production (maintains corpus luteum early on); (5) takes over oestrogen and progesterone production at ~12 weeks (corpus luteum then degenerates). IEB 2014: "Acts as a barrier against pathogens and drugs" = placenta.
Structures protecting the foetus
Weeks 4–40
Umbilical cord = connects foetus to placenta. Contains: 2 umbilical arteries (carry deoxygenated blood + CO₂ + waste FROM foetus to placenta) + 1 umbilical vein (carries oxygenated blood + nutrients TO foetus). IEB 2014: "Responsible for transport of foetal waste products" = umbilical cord.
Amnion = strong, smooth membrane forming the fluid-filled sac around the embryo. IEB 2014: "Strong membrane around embryo" = amnion.
Amniotic fluid = liquid filling the amniotic sac. Protects foetus from mechanical shock/impact; maintains temperature; allows foetus to move (essential for muscle and joint development). IEB 2014: "Substance that protects and cushions foetus from impact injury" = amniotic fluid.
Birth (Parturition)
~Week 40
Labour begins with uterine contractions. OXYTOCIN (posterior pituitary) intensifies contractions in a positive feedback loop (more contractions → more oxytocin → more contractions). Cervix dilates to 10 cm. Amniotic sac ruptures ("waters break"). Baby is born head-first (normally). Placenta expelled as "afterbirth". IEB 2014: "Labour initiated by contraction of certain muscles in this organ" = uterus.
📈 7. Secondary Sexual Characteristics at Puberty

Physical vs physiological changes — and the classic exam trap

Males — triggered by TESTOSTERONE

Physical changes: Voice deepens (larynx and vocal cords enlarge); facial, pubic, and underarm hair grows; penis and testes enlarge; shoulders broaden; increased muscle mass; acne (increased sebaceous gland activity); general growth spurt

⚠️ 3. PCOS — Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (IEB 2022 — 14 marks)

What PCOS is, how it differs from a normal cycle, and why it causes infertility

PCOS = a hormonal disorder in which the ovaries produce too many male hormones (androgens). This imbalance causes numerous small cysts (fluid-filled sacs) to form in the ovaries and disrupts the normal menstrual cycle. It affects 1 in 10 women of childbearing age (10%).

Symptoms of PCOS (IEB 2022 — list any TWO for 2 marks):

  • Missed or irregular menstrual periods (may be painful)
  • Infertility (no ovulation occurs)
  • Acne and increased body/facial hair (excess androgens cause this)
  • Weight gain
  • Depression and mood swings

  • Without the LH surge, no ovulation occurs → no egg is released → fertilisation cannot happen → INFERTILITY

Treatment (IEB 2022):

Oral contraceptive pill or hormonal patch → "decreases LH and FSH secretion to normalise the LH/FSH ratio." This regularises the cycle and reduces androgen production. Weight loss also improves symptoms. For fertility: clomiphene citrate (induces ovulation); IVF.

Social/cultural impact of PCOS (IEB 2022 — any 2 for 2 marks):

  • Painful periods may cause absenteeism from school/work
  • Cultural stigma around infertility / inability to conceive (especially in cultures where women are expected to have children)
  • Financial cost of treatment / assisted reproductive technology (IVF)
  • Emotional difficulties — mood swings/depression from hormonal imbalance

🌸 Flowering Plants
1. Explain clearly the difference between pollination and fertilisation in flowering plants. (4)
IEB 2014 model answer:
  • Pollination = the transfer of pollen from the anther to the stigma (of the same or different flower)
  • Fertilisation = the fusion of a male gamete (from pollen tube) with a female gamete (egg cell inside ovule)
  • Pollination leads to a pollen tube growing down the style to deliver the sperm nucleus to the ovule
  • Fertilisation occurs inside the ovule and produces a zygote which develops into the embryo
Marks awarded: 2 for pollination (transfer + anther-to-stigma) + 2 for fertilisation (fusion + gametes/zygote inside ovule)
2. Why is the tomato regarded as a fruit? (IEB 2020) (2)
Any 2 of:
  • It contains seeds
  • It developed from an ovary (ovary wall = pericarp)
  • Its seeds developed from ovules
  • It is a product of sexual reproduction
3. Explain why a farmer growing commercial peaches would choose asexual reproduction over seed production. Give ONE advantage and ONE disadvantage of this choice. (IEB 2014) (4)
Advantage (2 marks): "Cost-effective and time-saving — grafted/cloned saplings grow faster than seedlings, reaching productivity earlier. Product (peach) quality is consistent for food markets because all offspring are genetically identical."

Disadvantage (2 marks): "Reduction of genetic variation — can result in huge crop loss from disease/pests because all plants are equally susceptible (no variation to confer resistance). Relies on an artificial process with additional cost."
4. What is the importance of seed banks for ensuring food security? (IEB 2020) (2)
Any 2 of:
  • Important for storing seeds to re-establish crop plants in case of a natural disaster / drought / crop failure
  • To re-introduce genetic variation / biodiversity into cultivated plants that have become uniform through inbreeding
  • To store seeds from wild plant relatives for the future
5. Explain the role auxins play in asexual reproduction to produce many polyploid plants. (IEB 2020) (3)
  • Asexual reproduction involves causing plant somatic cells to produce new plant organs / new plants
  • Auxins cause cells to undergo mitosis (cell division)
  • Auxins cause differentiation — cells develop into root, stem, and leaf cells / produce a new plant
6. Suggest why self-pollination may easily occur in tomatoes. (IEB 2020) (2)
  • The stamens wrap around / form a tube around the stigma OR the stigma is shorter than the stamens / positioned below anthers
  • Making it easy for pollen to fall/reach the stigma which is surrounded by / below the anthers
🔬 Endocrine System
7. What is a hormone? Give a complete definition. (IEB 2014) (2)
  • A (protein/steroid) chemical messenger / metabolic regulator
  • Produced in one part of the body and controls/regulates the activity of another (target) organ; transported in the bloodstream
8. Describe the function of insulin in the human body. (IEB 2014) (2)
Any 2 connected points:
  • Lowers blood glucose levels
  • Makes cell membranes more permeable to glucose (allows glucose to enter cells)
  • Increases the rate at which glucose is converted to glycogen in liver and muscle cells (glycogenesis)
  • Stimulates cellular respiration (glucose burned for energy)
9. Name TWO symptoms each of Cretinism and Myxoedema, and give the cause of each disorder. (IEB 2013) (6)

Cretinism — Cause: hyposecretion of thyroxin in infants/children

  • Short/stunted stature
  • Slow mental development / intellectual disability
  • Thick skin / coarse facial features
  • Delayed puberty / immature sexual development

Myxoedema — Cause: hyposecretion of thyroxin in adults

  • Thickening of skin on hands/feet/face
  • Tongue thickens; slow physical and mental activities
  • Weight gain; low body temperature; constipation; fatigue; low heart rate
10. Draw a flow diagram to show negative feedback in the regulation of sperm production. Begin with: "Testosterone levels are low." (IEB 2019) (6)
  1. Testosterone levels are low → detected by hypothalamus
  2. Hypothalamus releases GnRH → stimulates anterior pituitary →
  3. Anterior pituitary releases LH (ICSH) and FSH →
  4. LH stimulates Leydig cells in testes to secrete more testosterone →
  5. FSH + testosterone stimulate spermatogenesis / sperm production →
  6. High testosterone feeds back to hypothalamus and pituitary → LH and FSH secretion decreases → testosterone returns to normal set point (NEGATIVE FEEDBACK)
Marking: 4+ correct sequential points with directional arrows.
11. What is the relationship between glucagon and blood sugar levels? (IEB 2014) (2)
  • As blood sugar (glucose) level decreases, glucagon level increases
  • As glucagon increases, blood sugar increases (glucagon stimulates glycogenolysis in liver)
  • As blood sugar rises back to normal, glucagon secretion decreases — negative feedback
🧬 Human Reproduction
12. Define semen. (IEB 2014) (2)
  • A fluid containing sperm cells AND secretions from the accessory glands (seminal vesicles, prostate, Cowper's gland)
  • The fluid is alkaline (to neutralise vaginal acidity)
13. Explain how a pregnancy will change the level of progesterone from what is shown in a normal menstrual cycle graph, and give the reason for this change. (IEB 2020) (3)
  • Progesterone level will remain high / will not decrease after day 28
  • Because: the embryo implants into the endometrium → chorionic villi produce HCG
  • HCG maintains the corpus luteum → corpus luteum continues producing progesterone → endometrium remains intact → no menstruation → pregnancy continues
14. By referring to FSH and LH levels, explain why women with PCOS may be infertile. (IEB 2022) (3)
  • LH levels remain consistently high / there is no LH surge or peak in PCOS
  • FSH levels remain very low in PCOS → follicle development is not properly stimulated
  • Without a normal FSH stimulus and LH surge, no ovulation occurs → no egg released → fertilisation cannot happen → infertility
15. Give TWO differences between the menstrual cycles of women with PCOS compared to regular cycles. (IEB 2022) (4)
Any 2 differences (each stated as a comparison, 2 marks per difference):
  • PCOS: no ovulation / follicles form cysts. Normal: ovulation occurs (~day 14, egg released)
  • PCOS: LH levels remain high (avg ~15 µg/L) / do not fluctuate. Normal: LH peaks on day 14 (~10 µg/L) then drops
  • PCOS: FSH remains very low (<5 µg/L). Normal: FSH peaks near day 14 (>10 µg/L)
  • PCOS: missed or irregular menstruation. Normal: regular menstruation every ~28 days
16. Give TWO differences between spermatogenesis and oogenesis. (4)
Any 2 differences stated as comparisons:
  • Products: Spermatogenesis produces 4 equal, functional sperm per primary spermatocyte. Oogenesis produces 1 functional egg + 2–3 non-functional polar bodies per primary oocyte.
  • Continuity: Spermatogenesis is continuous from puberty throughout life. Oogenesis is a cyclic process arrested at prophase I before birth; resumes one cycle per month.
  • Location: Spermatogenesis in seminiferous tubules of testes. Oogenesis in ovaries.
  • Cell division equality: Spermatogenesis = equal division (all 4 spermatids same size). Oogenesis = unequal (egg gets most cytoplasm; polar bodies are tiny).
17. The progesterone injection works as a contraceptive because it: A) stops development of egg cells and ovulation; B) prevents thickening of endometrium; C) obstructs sperm in the oviduct; D) stimulates sperm movement. (IEB 2013 MCQ) (2)
Answer: A
The progesterone injection suppresses LH secretion from the pituitary → no LH surge → no Graafian follicle rupture → no ovulation → egg is not released → fertilisation cannot occur. The PRIMARY mechanism is preventing ovulation. It also thickens cervical mucus (secondary effect) but the question asks for the main mechanism and "stops development of egg cells and ovulation" correctly describes the hormonal pathway.
18. Name the structure at the junction of the bladder and urethra that secretes fluid contributing to semen. Is this the seminal vesicle, prostate gland, ovary, or foreskin? (IEB 2013 MCQ) (1)
Answer: B — Prostate gland
The prostate gland wraps around the urethra at its junction with the bladder. The seminal vesicles are separate pouches — they open into the vas deferens just before the urethra but do NOT surround the urethra at the bladder junction.

📝 Key Definitions to Know Exactly

🌸 Plant terms

  • Pollination: transfer of pollen from anther to stigma
  • Fertilisation: fusion of male and female gametes to form a zygote
  • Seed: a developed fertilised ovule; contains embryo + food store + testa
  • Fruit: developed ovary wall (pericarp) surrounding one or more seeds
  • Germination: resumption of growth of embryo inside a dormant seed when conditions are favourable
  • Self-pollination: transfer of pollen from anther to stigma of the SAME flower/plant
  • Cross-pollination: transfer of pollen to stigma of a DIFFERENT plant of the same species

🔬 Endocrine terms

  • Hormone: chemical messenger produced in one area, transported in blood, controls target organ elsewhere
  • Homeostasis: maintenance of stable internal environment despite external changes
  • Negative feedback: response that reverses/reduces the original stimulus to restore set point
  • Glycogenesis: glucose → glycogen (insulin effect; storage)
  • Glycogenolysis: glycogen → glucose (glucagon/adrenalin effect; release)
  • Hyperglycaemia: blood glucose too high (diabetes)
  • Hypoglycaemia: blood glucose too low (dangerous)

🧬 Reproductive terms

  • Ovulation: release of secondary oocyte from ruptured Graafian follicle
  • Implantation: embedding of blastocyst into endometrium of uterus
  • Semen: fluid = sperm + secretions from accessory glands (alkaline)
  • Menstruation: shedding of endometrium when no implantation occurs
  • Spermatogenesis: production of sperm cells in seminiferous tubules
  • Oogenesis: production of egg cells in the ovaries
  • HCG: human chorionic gonadotropin — from chorionic villi; maintains corpus luteum; detected by pregnancy tests

🩺 Disorder shorthand

  • Cretinism: hypo-thyroxin → child → short + slow mental development
  • Myxoedema: hypo-thyroxin → adult → thick skin + weight gain + fatigue
  • Grave's Disease: hyper-thyroxin → adult → weight loss + exophthalmos + rapid heart rate
  • Goitre: iodine deficiency → neck swelling
  • Gigantism: hyper-GH → child → very tall

Trigonometrie

Afrikaans · Vraestel 2 · identiteite, vergelykings, grafieke & 2D/3D

📐 Identiteite & reëls

Basiese identiteite

  • sin²θ + cos²θ = 1
  • tan θ = sin θ ⁄ cos θ

Saamgestelde & dubbelhoeke

  • sin(A ± B) = sin A cos B ± cos A sin B
  • cos(A ± B) = cos A cos B ∓ sin A sin B
  • sin 2A = 2 sin A cos A
  • cos 2A = cos²A − sin²A = 1 − 2sin²A = 2cos²A − 1

Algemene oplossing

  • sin θ = sin α  ⇒  θ = α + 360°k  OF  θ = 180° − α + 360°k
  • cos θ = cos α  ⇒  θ = ± α + 360°k
  • tan θ = tan α  ⇒  θ = α + 180°k  (k ∈ ℤ)

Driehoeke (2D & 3D)

  • Sinusreël: a ⁄ sin A = b ⁄ sin B = c ⁄ sin C
  • Cosinusreël: a² = b² + c² − 2bc·cos A
  • Oppervlakreël: Oppervlak = ½·ab·sin C
